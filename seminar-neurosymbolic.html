Neurosymbolic AI in Military Systems

Seminar Presentation · 2025

This was a seminar I presented on Neurosymbolic AI (NeSy), especially where it could matter in defense and safety-critical systems. I focused on the basic question: can we combine learning from data with explicit reasoning and rules?

What is Neurosymbolic AI?

Neurosymbolic AI is the "third wave" of AI, merging two traditionally separate approaches:

  • Neural networks — excel at pattern recognition, learning from data, and handling noisy inputs (what Kahneman calls System 1 thinking)
  • Symbolic reasoning — uses explicit rules, logic, and knowledge representation for structured, explainable decision-making (System 2 thinking)

The appeal of NeSy is that it tries to use both: neural models for perception and pattern recognition, symbolic systems for rules, constraints, and explanations.

Why the military?

Military systems are a useful case study because mistakes are expensive and explanations matter. A neural network might detect something in an image, but that does not automatically explain why the result should be trusted or how it fits operational rules. NeSy is interesting because symbolic constraints can sit around neural predictions and make the decision process easier to inspect.

Key applications covered

  • Decision support under uncertainty — NeSy systems fuse noisy sensor data (radar, infrared, SIGINT) with symbolic doctrine to recommend courses of action with explicit reasoning chains
  • Explainability and audit — Every prediction can be traced back to the rules and data that produced it, enabling after-action review and legal accountability
  • Multi-modal sensor fusion — Neural networks handle raw sensor streams; symbolic layers align detections across modalities using domain ontologies
  • Autonomous systems — Rules of engagement encoded as symbolic constraints ensure autonomous platforms operate within ethical and legal boundaries
  • Logistics optimization — Neural forecasting combined with symbolic constraint solving for supply chain and troop movement planning

Ethical and strategic considerations

The topic also raises difficult questions. Who is accountable when an autonomous system is wrong? How do you handle adversarial inputs? Can human judgment or ethics really be captured as rules? I treated explainability as the main reason NeSy is worth studying here, not as a complete solution to those problems.

Broader relevance

Even though the seminar focused on defense, the same pattern shows up in civilian systems too: healthcare, fraud detection, autonomous vehicles, and other areas where a model needs to be both useful and explainable. The broader lesson for me was that safety-critical AI cannot rely only on black-box prediction.

Main reference: arXiv:2408.09224 — "Neuro-Symbolic AI for Military Applications" (Hagos & Rawat, IEEE TAI 2024)
